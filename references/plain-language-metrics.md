# Plain-Language Metrics

Use this reference when the report should be readable by non-specialist readers.

## Goal

Define key metrics in plain language and explain why they matter to the conclusion.

## Writing pattern

For each important metric:
1. say what it is
2. say what it is useful for
3. say what it does not prove on its own
4. connect it back to the company’s business model

## Reusable explanations

### Revenue
- **What it is**: the money the company booked from its business activities during the period.
- **Why it matters**: it shows the scale of each business line.
- **What it does not prove**: a larger revenue segment is not automatically the most profitable or the highest-quality segment.

### EBITDA
- **What it is**: operating earnings before depreciation and amortisation.
- **Why it matters**: it helps show operating cash-generation before heavy asset charges.
- **What it does not prove**: it is not the same as sustainable profit for an asset-heavy company.

### EBIT
- **What it is**: operating profit after depreciation and amortisation.
- **Why it matters**: it shows how much operating profit remains after recognising asset consumption.
- **What it does not prove**: a weak EBIT does not always mean the segment lacks strategic value or operating cash-generation.

### Net debt-to-equity ratio
- **What it is**: net debt divided by equity.
- **Why it matters**: it is a simple way to read financial leverage.
- **What it does not prove**: a lower ratio does not always mean the company repaid debt; equity growth or hybrid capital can also lower it.

### Interest cover
- **What it is**: a measure of how easily operating earnings can cover financing costs.
- **Why it matters**: it helps show whether current earnings support the debt burden.
- **What it does not prove**: a healthy ratio today does not remove long-cycle capex or refinancing risk.

### CFO / operating cash flow
- **What it is**: the cash generated from normal operations.
- **Why it matters**: it helps show whether accounting earnings are turning into real cash.
- **What it does not prove**: strong operating cash flow does not automatically mean low future capex needs.

### FCF / free cash flow
- **What it is**: operating cash flow after capital expenditure.
- **Why it matters**: it helps show how much cash remains after funding the asset base.
- **What it does not prove**: a weak FCF in one period may reflect growth or renewal investment rather than weak business quality.
