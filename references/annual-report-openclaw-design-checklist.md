# Annual Report OpenClaw Design Checklist

Use this checklist when generating a company annual-report-style analysis in OpenClaw, especially when the goal is not just to describe the company, but to explain change, trend, and competitive position.

## Core principle

Weight the report around three questions:
1. **What changed?**
2. **Why did it change?**
3. **How does the company compare with similar businesses?**

Do not let the report become a static description of current numbers only.

## 1. Revenue section

- [ ] Do not stop at segment revenue levels; explain what changed in the latest period.
- [ ] When discussing 2025 revenue or recent-period revenue, identify major projects, contracts, launches, expansions, openings, or business events that contributed materially where public evidence exists.
- [ ] Search beyond the annual report when necessary:
  - [ ] official press releases
  - [ ] results announcements
  - [ ] investor presentations
  - [ ] credible public reports
  - [ ] public news coverage used only after source/date validation
- [ ] If a project/event is used to explain revenue, check:
  - [ ] event date
  - [ ] reporting period
  - [ ] whether the event is a real revenue driver or just management narrative

## 2. Historical comparison and trend

- [ ] For each key indicator, ask whether a historical comparison is needed to show change clearly.
- [ ] Prefer 2-3 comparable periods over a single isolated point.
- [ ] Highlight the direction and magnitude of change, not just the latest number.
- [ ] For cost items such as staff costs and related expenses, show:
  - [ ] absolute change
  - [ ] ratio to revenue where useful
  - [ ] why this matters to profit or margin

## 3. Station commercial / rental-management / recurring income blocks

- [ ] For recurring businesses, do not only state that they are “high-quality”; show why.
- [ ] Show at least:
  - [ ] current scale
  - [ ] year-on-year growth rate or comparable trend
  - [ ] contribution to total revenue or total recurring earnings
- [ ] Explain what drove the increase or decline:
  - [ ] occupancy
  - [ ] traffic / footfall
  - [ ] pricing
  - [ ] project openings
  - [ ] asset additions
  - [ ] macro weakness
- [ ] If citing large projects or events as drivers, verify them with date-aware sources.

## 4. Unit / currency / dimensional consistency

- [ ] Every data point pulled from news, reports, or public materials must be checked for:
  - [ ] unit
  - [ ] currency
  - [ ] period type
  - [ ] whether the number is flow or point-in-time
- [ ] Do not compare metrics unless they are on the same dimensional basis.
- [ ] Normalize units and labels before charting or tabulation.

## 5. External source discipline

- [ ] External search or news APIs may be used for discovery, but not as final evidence when official sources are available.
- [ ] For news-derived claims, check both:
  - [ ] event date
  - [ ] report / publication date
- [ ] Be careful not to use a recent article to explain an earlier period unless the timing makes sense.
- [ ] If a source is recent but the financial effect belongs to a different period, explain the lag explicitly.

## 6. KPI selection

- [ ] Do not overload the report with too many indicators.
- [ ] Prefer 5-6 key indicators that best explain the company’s model.
- [ ] For each chosen KPI, focus on:
  - [ ] trend
  - [ ] change driver
  - [ ] peer context where available
- [ ] A weaker but well-explained KPI set is better than a long but shallow list.

## 7. Single-point KPI problem

- [ ] Do not present 2025-only indicators without asking what changed versus prior periods.
- [ ] If only one period is available, mark the limitation and avoid over-interpreting the number.
- [ ] Wherever possible, pair the latest metric with:
  - [ ] prior-period comparison
  - [ ] management explanation
  - [ ] financing / strategy context

## 8. Peer / industry comparison

- [ ] Add peer comparison when it materially improves interpretation.
- [ ] Classify the target company properly before choosing peers.
- [ ] If the company is hybrid, tag by business line rather than forcing one single peer bucket.
- [ ] Compare against sector leaders or relevant benchmark operators only when the business model is comparable enough.
- [ ] For each comparison, explain:
  - [ ] where the company is stronger
  - [ ] where it is weaker
  - [ ] why the gap exists
  - [ ] whether the advantage is sustainable

## 9. Future potential and strategic position

- [ ] The conclusion should not only say what the company earns today.
- [ ] Summarize:
  - [ ] what changed recently
  - [ ] what trend is visible
  - [ ] what future potential or pressure exists
  - [ ] what position the company occupies in the industry
- [ ] Distinguish clearly between:
  - [ ] current reported performance
  - [ ] pipeline / future projects
  - [ ] strategic optionality
  - [ ] management aspiration

## 10. Writing emphasis

- [ ] Put more weight on three dimensions:
  - [ ] change
  - [ ] trend
  - [ ] comparison
- [ ] Do not let the report remain a flat inventory of current facts.
- [ ] Each major section should answer at least one of these:
  - [ ] what changed?
  - [ ] why?
  - [ ] relative to whom?
  - [ ] what does it imply for the future?

## Final delivery rule

A strong deliverable should let the reader understand:
- the business model today
- the biggest recent changes
- the key reasons behind those changes
- how the company compares with relevant peers
- whether the current advantages or weaknesses are likely to persist
