# Peer Comparison

Use this reference when the user asks how a company differs from peers, what its operating model reveals relative to peers, or whether a business is more conservative / more leveraged / more asset-heavy than competitors.

## Goal

Use peer comparison to explain business-model differences, not to dump unrelated ratios.

## Minimum workflow

1. Define the comparison question first.
2. Choose a small peer set that shares enough business logic to be informative.
3. Keep metrics on a like-for-like basis.
4. Explain what the comparison means for the target company’s operating model.
5. If comparability is weak, downgrade the section to a qualified framing note.

## Good comparison questions

- Is the target company more dependent on recurring regulated or concession-style income than peers?
- Does the target company rely more on property, retail, or station monetisation than peers?
- Is leverage lower because of true deleveraging, stronger equity, or hybrid capital treatment?
- Does the company earn from traffic, asset ownership, development gains, or management contracts in a different mix from peers?

## Peer selection rule

Prefer 2-4 peers. Avoid large peer baskets unless the task is explicitly quantitative screening.

Choose peers by business logic, not ticker familiarity alone. For MTR-like work, possible peers may include:
- rail / metro operators with commercial monetisation
- transport-infrastructure operators with property-linked monetisation
- transit or concession operators with long-cycle capex and hybrid financing

If no true peer exists, say so. A narrower but honest peer set is better than a broad but misleading one.

## Comparability rule

Before comparing, align:
- period type: FY vs H1 vs quarter
- metric level: revenue vs EBITDA vs EBIT vs net profit vs balance-sheet metrics
- unit and currency
- segment definitions
- recurring versus development / one-off profit

Do not compare a full-year target with interim peer data as if it were a clean cross-sectional conclusion.

## Recommended comparison skeleton

### 1. Comparison purpose
State in one sentence why these peers were chosen.

### 2. Shared business logic
State what the target and peers have in common.

### 3. Key difference table
Use a compact table such as:
- company
- main revenue engine
- recurring monetisation layer
- role of property / development
- leverage / financing style
- what makes it different

### 4. Focused metric comparison
Pick only 1-3 metric families, for example:
- revenue mix
- recurring margin proxy
- leverage and liquidity
- capex intensity

### 5. Interpretation
End with what the comparison reveals about the target company’s operating model.

## Output rule

A good peer-comparison section should answer:
- Why these peers?
- What is actually comparable?
- What does the difference mean?

A bad peer-comparison section merely lists ratios with no business interpretation.

## For MTR-like cases

Useful framing angles include:
- extent of non-fare monetisation
- dependence on property-related earnings versus transport earnings
- whether balance-sheet conservatism comes from cash generation, equity base, or hybrid capital
- degree of exposure to international contract / concession operations versus local core assets
