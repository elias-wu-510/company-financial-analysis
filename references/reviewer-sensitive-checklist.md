# Reviewer-sensitive financial report checklist

Use this checklist when the reviewer is likely to inspect metric comparability, evidence discipline, wording precision, and formatting consistency in detail.

## 1. Comparability

- Do not rank or judge segments using mixed metric levels.
- Do not present revenue, EBITDA, EBIT, recurrent EBIT, post-tax profit, and point-in-time balance-sheet metrics as if they were directly comparable.
- If mixed metrics must appear together for explanatory context, add an explicit warning that they are not directly comparable and explain the role of each metric.
- Do not compare FY data with H1 or quarter data as if they were direct year-on-year equivalents.

## 2. Evidence discipline

- Attach nearby evidence to every non-trivial judgment.
- Avoid hard claims such as "weakened", "shifted", "improved resilience", or "depended more on local assets" unless directly supported by disclosed figures or cited management commentary.
- If support is partial, downgrade the language to a qualified interpretation.

## 3. Driver specificity

- Replace generic statements such as "costs increased" with the most specific disclosed driver.
- When explaining margin compression, identify whether it came from staff cost, depreciation, maintenance, financing cost, lease-style payments, concession payments, or mix changes.
- When explaining leverage improvement, distinguish debt repayment from equity increase, perpetual capital securities, hybrid instruments, cash build, or accounting presentation effects.

## 4. Language, unit, and label consistency

- Use one narrative language throughout.
- Use one unit convention throughout.
- Use one period-labeling convention throughout.
- Make chart titles explicit about period, unit, and metric level.

## 5. Reader clarity

- Define key metrics in plain language on first use.
- Explain why the metric matters to the conclusion.
- Write so a non-specialist reader can follow the logic without prior company knowledge.

## 6. Chart discipline

- Let each chart answer one clear question.
- Remove charts that duplicate the same analytical point.
- Avoid decorative charts that do not support a specific claim.

## 7. Operating-model analysis

- Explain how operating segments interact, not just what each segment reported.
- Distinguish recurring earnings engines from timing-sensitive development or disposal gains.
- For transport, infrastructure, property-linked, or other asset-heavy businesses, explain the business logic linking traffic, commercial monetisation, property, and financing.
