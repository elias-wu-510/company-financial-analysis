# Driver Analysis

Use this reference when the report needs to explain why a metric changed, not merely state that it changed.

## Goal

Move from:
- revenue increased
- margin declined
- leverage improved

To:
- what changed
- why it changed
- which driver mattered most
- whether the driver is operating, financing, accounting, or one-off

## General rule

Every important conclusion about change should be traced to one or more concrete drivers from the filings.

Avoid vague explanations such as:
- costs increased
- the market was weak
- profitability improved
- leverage got better

Replace them with named drivers.

## Driver buckets

### 1. Revenue drivers

Check whether the change came from:
- volume / passenger trips / customer count / shipment / usage
- price / tariff / ticket fare / ARPU / yield
- product or segment mix
- geography or region mix
- project timing or completion timing
- currency / FX translation
- acquisitions, disposals, or contract changes

### 2. Margin and profit drivers

Check whether the change came from:
- staff costs and related expenses
- maintenance / repair / engineering / service costs
- raw materials / energy / utilities
- depreciation and amortisation
- rent, concession, or lease-style payments
- selling and administrative expenses
- financing costs
- impairment, write-down, or fair-value changes
- one-off gains or losses
- shift in segment mix toward lower- or higher-margin activities

### 3. Leverage and balance-sheet drivers

Check whether the change came from:
- actual debt repayment
- new borrowing
- cash accumulation or cash burn
- equity issuance
- hybrid / perpetual capital instruments
- asset sales
- working-capital movements
- accounting reclassification or presentation effects

### 4. Operating-model drivers

When the task is about operating model, identify:
- what creates demand / traffic / user flow
- what converts that flow into monetisation
- what generates recurring income
- what acts as the profit amplifier in strong years
- what requires the financing structure to work

## Priority rule

Do not list every possible driver mechanically.

Instead:
1. identify the metric that changed
2. identify 1-3 drivers that most plausibly explain the move from official disclosures
3. state which one appears primary
4. note if the move is partly accounting or financing-driven rather than operational

## Writing pattern

A useful pattern is:

1. State the change.
2. Name the main driver.
3. Add one supporting detail from the filing.
4. Explain what this means for the business model or quality of earnings.

Example structure:
- EBIT margin fell by X.
- The main driver was higher staff costs and related expenses, with maintenance spending also increasing.
- This matters because the segment has high fixed-cost intensity, so modest revenue growth does not automatically convert into profit growth.

## Escalation rule

If a report says any of the following, force a driver check before finalising:
- margin declined / improved
- leverage improved / worsened
- segment contribution changed
- earnings quality improved / weakened
- recurring profit strengthened / weakened

## If the filing is incomplete

If the exact driver cannot be confirmed from official documents:
- say the driver appears likely, but mark it as tentative
- do not overstate certainty
- prefer naming the confirmed bucket (for example, staff costs, depreciation, or hybrid capital issuance) over inventing a precise story
